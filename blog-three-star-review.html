The Three-Star Review You Didn't See Coming

Delivery·2 min read

Beth Whitfield's estate was genuinely complicated — a blended family, a disputed asset, a will that hadn't been updated in fifteen years. The firm handling it did careful, correct work. Every beneficiary was eventually paid exactly what they were owed.

The engagement letter had promised monthly updates. Between two staff changes and a busy period, the executor — the deceased's eldest daughter, already exhausted from managing her mother's affairs — didn't hear anything for nearly two months during the administration.

She didn't complain. She assumed no news meant nothing was happening, and quietly began to dread every phone call she had to make herself just to find out where things stood. Each time she rang, she got the same reply: it's in progress.

The estate settled correctly, eventually. Three weeks later, she left a review: three stars. “Got there in the end, but I had to chase them constantly. Felt like my mum's estate wasn't a priority.”

The firm's actual work was never the problem. What she remembered — what she told her own friends when the subject came up — was her experience, the two months of silence in the middle.

A correct result and a good client experience are not the same thing. Only one story gets told to the next person asking for a recommendation. Most lawyers get frustrated in this situation; improving your delivery process is a better use of your time.
