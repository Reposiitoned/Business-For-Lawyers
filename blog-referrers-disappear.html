What Happens When Your Two Best Referrers Both Disappear in the Same Year

Marketing·2 min read

For eleven years, Tim's family law practice ran almost entirely on two relationships — a financial planner who sent him separating couples working through settlements, and a mortgage broker who referred anyone navigating a difficult custody matter. He never built a website worth mentioning. He didn't need to. The two of them accounted for most of his new files, year after year.

Then, within four months of each other, the planner sold his practice and moved to the coast, and the mortgage broker decided to retire, earlier than expected.

Tim still did excellent work. Nothing about his ability had changed. But the phone, which had rung reliably for over a decade, went quiet in a way he'd never experienced.

He spent the next year rebuilding — a proper website, a monthly note to past clients, a relationship with a new set of referrers. It worked, eventually. But it took twelve months of rebuilding what could have been growing quietly in the background the entire time.

Referral relationships aren't a marketing strategy. They're two people — and people retire, relocate, and change their minds. The firms that keep growing treat referrals as one input, not the entire pipeline.
